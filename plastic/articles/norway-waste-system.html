Norway's Recycling System Works Better Than Many Think

By Bror Tobias Rinde — March 18, 2026

The bigger problem is how much recyclable waste still ends up in the residual bin

Public conversations about waste and plastic pollution often give the impression that recycling hardly works. Norwegian data tell a different story. The national waste accounts show that several common household materials are recycled at high rates when they are sorted correctly. The main weakness is not that the entire system fails, but that too much recyclable waste is still thrown into mixed residual waste [1].

Households matter in the national waste picture

In 2023, Norway generated 11.1 million tonnes of waste in total. Private households accounted for 2.167 million tonnes of this, making households the second-largest waste source after construction and demolition activities. SSB's 2023 waste accounts also show that construction and demolition generated about 2.699 million tonnes that year [2].

That matters because household habits are not a marginal issue. What people put in the right bin, and what they do not, has consequences at national scale.

A large share of household waste is still residual waste

SSB's material breakdown for private households shows that the largest category in 2023 was mixed residual waste at 851,000 tonnes, about 39 percent of all household waste. In the same year, households also sorted substantial amounts of other waste streams, including 219,000 tonnes of wet organic waste, 191,000 tonnes of paper and cardboard, 76,000 tonnes of glass, 86,000 tonnes of metal, and 50,000 tonnes of plastic [3].

This is an important starting point. Norwegian households are already sorting large amounts of waste, but the residual bin still receives a very large share.

The data show that sorting works

The strongest argument for sorting is what happens after waste enters the correct stream. According to SSB's 2023 waste accounts, 521,000 of 577,000 tonnes of paper waste were materially recycled, equal to about 90 percent. For glass, 139,000 of 145,000 tonnes were materially recycled, or nearly 96 percent. For metal, 532,000 of 533,000 tonnes were materially recycled, which is effectively 100 percent. Plastic had a lower rate than glass and metal, but still 112,000 of 160,000 tonnes were materially recycled, corresponding to 70 percent [4].

Organic waste also shows that sorted waste can be used productively. Out of 618,000 tonnes of wet organic waste, 294,000 tonnes went to biogas production, 221,000 tonnes to material recycling, and 71,000 tonnes to composting. Only 26,000 tonnes were incinerated [4].

These figures make one point very clear: for many ordinary household materials, sorting is not symbolic. It has measurable effects.

The residual bin is where recyclable value is lost

The picture changes sharply when waste is placed in the mixed residual stream. In 2023, Norway generated 2.711 million tonnes of residual waste. Of this, 2.149 million tonnes, or about 79 percent, went to incineration. Another 241,000 tonnes went to landfill. Only 234,000 tonnes, around 8.6 percent, were materially recycled [4].

This is why correct sorting matters so much. A cardboard package, a metal container, or a recyclable plastic item can have a relatively high chance of being recovered when sorted correctly. But once it enters the residual stream, it is much more likely to be burned than recycled [4].

More than half of residual waste may not belong there

This is reinforced by the waste composition analysis presented by Kim Mjønestrø in the SirkPlast project. In the presentation's average of two analyses, only 48.3 percent of the examined waste was classified as actual residual waste. The remaining 51.7 percent consisted of other fractions, including wood at 21.2 percent, metal at 9.4 percent, paper and cardboard at 7.1 percent, plastic fractions, and other materials.

This finding is central. It suggests that the problem is not only what treatment plants do with waste after collection. A major part of the problem begins earlier, when recyclable materials are placed in the wrong bin.

Incineration does not make waste disappear

Residual waste in Norway is largely incinerated, often with energy recovery. But incineration is not the same as elimination. Store norske leksikon states that energy recovery from 4 tonnes of waste produces about 1 tonne of residual products. These include bottom ash, slag, filter dust, and sludge from flue-gas cleaning. The same source notes that residues from the cleaning process are hazardous waste, and that bottom ash and slag are also treated as hazardous waste unless it can be documented otherwise [5].

So when recyclable items are thrown into residual waste, they do not simply vanish. They are more likely to be converted into heat, emissions, ash, and contaminated residues that still need long-term management [5].

What the Norwegian data actually show

Taken together, the evidence gives a more precise picture than the common claim that “recycling does not work.” For several everyday materials, Norway's waste system performs well when waste is sorted correctly. Paper, glass, and metal stand out especially clearly, and even plastic shows a much higher material-recycling share than many people assume [4].

The key weakness is the large quantity of recyclable material that still enters the mixed residual stream. When that happens, the probability of material recovery falls sharply and the probability of incineration rises just as sharply [5].

Why this matters for microplastics

This wider perspective is important because waste handling is not only about efficiency. It is also about pollution pathways. Plastic that is poorly sorted, mixed with other waste, degraded during handling, or lost from the waste chain has more opportunities to fragment over time. That increases the risk of long-term plastic pollution, including microplastics.

Better sorting alone will not solve the microplastic problem. But the Norwegian data suggest that it is still one of the simplest and most concrete steps the public can take: keep recyclable materials, especially plastics, out of the residual bin, so they are less likely to be burned, broken down, or lost from the system.
