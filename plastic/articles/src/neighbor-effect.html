One of the most influential behavioral field experiments of the past two decades involved something remarkably simple: sending households a monthly letter comparing their electricity use to that of their neighbors. Across nearly 600,000 households, this intervention reduced electricity consumption by approximately two percent on average. Among the highest users, reductions reached more than six percent. The effect was equivalent to a short-term electricity price increase of ten to eleven percent. Most strikingly, when the reports stopped, consumption gradually returned to previous levels. When the reports continued, the reduction persisted.(Allcott, 2011). (European Institute for Energy Research, 2020). The mechanism behind this change was not financial punishment or regulation. It was social comparison. Humans are deeply responsive to norms. When individuals learn that they consume more than their peers, many adjust their behavior downward. When they learn they are performing well, they often maintain that standard. Social feedback activates competitiveness, reputation concerns, and a desire for alignment with perceived community standards. (European Institute for Energy Research, 2020). Modern retail infrastructure makes it possible to apply this principle to plastic consumption. Loyalty programs already track purchasing patterns in detail. Retailers know which products customers buy, how frequently they buy them, and what packaging those products contain. With a standardized measurement framework for packaging weight and material type, retailers could generate monthly plastic footprint reports for consumers. Imagine receiving a notification stating that your household used eighteen percent more plastic packaging than similar households this month, along with suggestions for lower-plastic alternatives. Alternatively, imagine being informed that you are among the top twenty percent of low-plastic shoppers in your area. These subtle comparisons would not impose penalties. They would provide information and activate social norms. Evidence suggests that even a two percent behavioral shift is realistic under this type of intervention. Across national retail chains, that shift would represent a substantial reduction in plastic waste. Importantly, continuous feedback is essential. Just as energy savings faded when reports stopped, plastic reductions would require repeated, periodic reminders. Behavioral change is maintained through reinforcement. Governments can support this approach by encouraging standardized plastic measurement systems and incentivizing retailers to integrate sustainability dashboards into their digital platforms. Businesses, in turn, can position themselves as leaders in transparency while simultaneously reducing environmental impact. Social comparison is not coercive, but it is powerful. When behavior becomes visible, norms begin to move. **Allcott, H. (2011)**. *Social norms and energy conservation*. **Journal of Public Economics**, 95(9-10).
