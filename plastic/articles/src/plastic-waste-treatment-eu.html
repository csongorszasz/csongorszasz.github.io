The current state of plastic waste treatment in the EU reveals a mixture of progress and ongoing challenges. In 2022, the total plastic packaging waste produced in the EU was 16.16 million tonnes. Each person living in the EU generated an average of 36.1 kilos of plastic packaging waste in 2022, which represents an increase of almost 8 kilos per person since 2012. How this waste is handled breaks down as follows. Recycling is the most used method for disposing of plastic waste in Europe, accounting for 40.7% (or 6.58 million tonnes) of the total. The second most utilized method is energy recovery, which treats 35.0% of plastic waste through processes like incineration to create usable heat, electricity, or fuel. About 1.3 million tonnes of plastic waste were exported outside the EU in 2023 for treatment. Nearly half of the plastic waste collected for recycling in the EU is exported, often due to a lack of domestic capacity, technology, or financial resources to process it locally. Recycling rates vary significantly across member states. In 2022, Slovakia recycled 59.6% of its plastic packaging waste, Belgium recycled 54.2%, and Germany recycled 51.1%. In contrast, other nations lagged significantly, such as Malta, which recycled only 16.4%. Ultimately, despite improvements in recycling rates, the majority of plastic waste in Europe is still either incinerated, landfilled, or exported.

What are the bottlenecks?

There are substantial economic, technical, and infrastructural barriers preventing a truly circular plastic economy in Europe. Plastic is highly variable and easily customized for specific functional or aesthetic needs, which complicates the recycling process, makes it costly, and affects the quality of the end product. Multi-material packaging, dark plastics that confuse sorting machines, and non-standard labelling severely undermine recycling efforts at scale. Recycled plastic degrades and varies depending on its source, whereas virgin plastic made from fossil fuels is often cheaper and offers more consistent performance. Consequently, manufacturers needing high-specification materials, such as food-grade packaging, frequently cannot rely on recycled plastic to meet functional or regulatory standards. There is a lack of cohesive investment in sorting technologies and limited interoperability among waste collection systems. Recycling infrastructure is also unevenly distributed across the continent; while some regions feature advanced optical sorting and chemical recycling, others are still dependent on basic mechanical processing or exporting their waste. Low levels of consumer awareness in many regions continue to amplify these structural barriers.

Consumer awareness in the EU

While the desire for sustainable options is strong, the reality of consumer behavior is heavily influenced by economic pressures, convenience, and a growing skepticism toward corporate sustainability claims. Packaging is actively driving purchasing decisions. Recent industry surveys indicate that over a third of European consumers have switched brands specifically due to concerns over packaging. Non-recyclable materials and excessive plastic are the leading culprits. Across the bloc, an overwhelming majority of consumers express a strong desire to buy products that use as little plastic packaging as possible. Consumers are becoming highly educated on circularity. A 2026 Amcor survey of ~3200 participants found that 91% of EU shoppers are aware of post-consumer recycled (PCR) plastic in supermarket packaging, and over 75% actively purchase these products, recognizing them as a vital way to reduce carbon footprints. Unfortunately, even with high awareness, hygiene concerns, shelf-life necessities, and sheer convenience still sometimes override environmental intentions at the point of purchase. While environmental concern is deeply entrenched, inflation and the cost of living remain significant pain points. Consumers in countries like Italy and Germany show a higher willingness to pay a premium for sustainable packaging, but for the broader EU public, sustainable alternatives must remain practical and affordable to achieve mass adoption.
