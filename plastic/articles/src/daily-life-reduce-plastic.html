Changing habits can feel overwhelming…but it doesn't have to be. By starting small you make the task at hand much more perceivable and manageable. Most people start with one or two choices and then let the rest grow naturally. The idea here is to:
  1. Recognise where plastic shows up in our daily life
  2. Choose a relevant action
  3. Try it for a while
  4. Repeat
and before you know it your home is healthier and your plastic consumption is reduced.

Recognising where to start and relevant action

It can be hard to recognise where to start and what action to take. The following is a simple guide to help you with that and hopefully help you change your habits for the better.

Simple swaps

Metal water bottle Lasts longer, keeps drinks clean and avoids plastic particles from worn bottles. Glass or steel food containers Better for storing and reheating food, especially warm meals. Beeswax wraps or silicone lids A reusable alternative to cling film that keeps food fresh without plastic waste. Cast iron or stainless steel pans Durable, easy to maintain and free from plastic based non-stick coatings. Reusable shopping bag A small habit that quickly reduces the amount of plastic entering your home.

In the kitchen

Wooden or bamboo cutting boards Plastic boards wear down over time and can shed tiny pieces into food. Silicone or wooden utensils Gentle on cookware and free from plastic coatings that degrade with heat. Buy loose fruits and vegetables Reduces packaging waste and often keeps produce fresher. Store leftovers in glass Glass doesn't absorb smells, stain, or release microplastics. Avoid heating food in plastic Heat can cause plastics to break down and release particles into your meal. Choose bulk foods when possible Less packaging and often better value.

In the bathroom

Bar soap Minimal packaging and long lasting. A simple way to cut down on plastic bottles. Bamboo toothbrush A small daily item that adds up over time when swapped for a biodegradable option. Natural exfoliants Scrubs with microbeads contain tiny plastics oats, sugar, or coffee grounds work just as well. Metal safety razor Durable, cost effective and avoids disposable plastic handles.

Laundry & clothing

Microfiber catching laundry bag Helps trap tiny plastic fibers released from synthetic clothes. Lower wash temperatures Gentler on fabrics and reduces shedding. Choose natural fabrics when possible Cotton, wool and linen shed fewer microplastics than synthetics. Detergent sheets or powder Comes in cardboard packaging and avoids large plastic bottles. Air-dry clothes Reduces wear and tear and therefor fewer fibers released.

Smart shopping

Bring your own bags Simple, reliable and reduces single use plastics. Choose cardboard, glass or metal packaging These materials are easier to recycle and don't break down into microplastics. Buy in bulk Less packaging and often cheaper. Pick whole foods Fresh foods usually come with less plastic and fewer additives. Refill when possible Soap, detergent, spices and grains can often be refilled without new packaging.
