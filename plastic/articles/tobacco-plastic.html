What Tobacco Policy Can Teach Us About Reducing Plastic Consumption

By Olve Grønås Birkeland — March 2, 2026

When the United States tested pictorial cigarette warnings in a randomized study of 2,149 smokers, the results looked modest at first. Thirty-four percent of smokers who received text-only warnings attempted to quit, compared to forty percent who received pictorial warnings. A six-percentage-point difference may not sound dramatic, but across millions of smokers, that small shift translates into hundreds of thousands of additional quit attempts. (Brewer et al., 2016).

That is the key lesson: small behavioral changes, when scaled across an entire population, create large societal impact.

Research from dozens of countries shows that stronger warning labels consistently increase knowledge and often increase quit attempts and reduce smoking prevalence. The reason is simple. Warnings interrupt automatic behavior. They make invisible costs visible. And when people are repeatedly exposed to those signals, norms begin to shift. (Noar et al., 2016)

Plastic consumption today resembles smoking decades ago. It is normalized, habitual, and rarely questioned at the point of purchase. Consumers do not see the long-term environmental consequences when picking up a product wrapped in plastic. They see convenience.

This is where plastic warning labels could make a real difference — especially on products where clear alternatives already exist.

Unlike smoking, plastic use is not addictive. Replacing plastic cutlery with reusable alternatives, choosing cardboard packaging instead of plastic, or selecting unpackaged produce does not require overcoming chemical dependency. In many cases, the substitute is just as convenient and similarly priced.

Because switching costs are low, warning labels on high-plastic products could have an even stronger behavioral effect than tobacco warnings. When a product clearly states how much single-use plastic it contains, how long it persists in the environment, or how it compares to lower-plastic options, consumers are more likely to reconsider.

This approach would be most effective on products where alternatives are obvious: disposable cutlery, single-use cups, excessive packaging, and plastic-wrapped goods that could easily be sold in cardboard or without packaging.

Even a three to five percent reduction in plastic-heavy purchases would generate significant environmental gains at national scale. Policymakers often underestimate incremental change because they focus on individual decisions rather than aggregated impact. Tobacco control shows that modest shifts can produce substantial public benefit.

Plastic policy can apply the same principle: make the cost visible, repeat the signal, and focus on areas where change is easy.

Behavior changes most where the resistance is lowest.
